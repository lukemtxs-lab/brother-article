The undervalued asset in family life: brotherly love

By JFK JR

In a world increasingly preoccupied with individual success and personal branding, the quiet economics of family relationships can be overlooked. Yet among the most enduring and undervalued of these bonds is the relationship between sisters and brothers — a connection that shapes confidence, resilience and, in many cases, a sense of lifelong security.

Sociologists have long noted that siblings form the earliest networks most people ever experience. Unlike friendships or professional alliances, these relationships are not chosen but inherited. They are, in effect, the original social contract. For sisters in particular, having a brother often brings an unusual combination of rivalry, protection and companionship that can prove quietly formative.

A brother’s presence can act as a buffer against the anxieties of adolescence. The sibling dynamic creates a space where teasing and disagreement coexist with loyalty. Many women recall brothers as their first sparring partners in debate, their first defenders in schoolyard disputes, and occasionally the first men to model what trust and respect might look like.

The practical benefits extend further than nostalgia. Studies into sibling relationships suggest that siblings who maintain strong bonds in adulthood often enjoy better emotional resilience and wider support networks. A brother can serve as a sounding board for career choices, a co-conspirator in family diplomacy and, occasionally, the person who knows precisely which childhood stories should never reach the public domain.

But perhaps the most important function of brotherly love lies in its constancy. While friends drift and careers move cities, siblings remain a fixed point. They remember the version of you that existed before professional titles, mortgages and adult responsibilities. In a society where identity is often fluid, that continuity has value.

For sisters, this relationship deserves particular appreciation. Brothers may not always articulate affection in the language of sentiment, but loyalty often appears through actions rather than words: turning up when needed, offering practical help, or simply being present during life’s more chaotic moments.

If the bond between sisters and brothers is an emotional investment, then it follows that it should occasionally yield a dividend in the form of appreciation.

And while gratitude can take many forms — a call, a thoughtful gesture, perhaps even letting him win an argument once in a while — there is also something to be said for the occasional well-chosen gift.
